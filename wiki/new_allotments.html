New Allotment Sites

Providing new allotment sites for local food growing. (Grant: CGS) (Sources: LNRS, NE GIDG 4.14, GI Design Guide - Food Growing).

Key Benefits (Derived from GI Guide principles)

  • Social & Community: Provides space for food growing; Encourages healthy eating & outdoor activity; Fosters community interaction & knowledge sharing; Educational opportunities.
  • Environmental: Reduces food miles; Potential for composting/waste reduction; Can contribute to local climate regulation & air quality (depending on scale/management).
  • Biodiversity: Can support pollinators and soil invertebrates if managed sympathetically; Potential for habitat creation (hedges, ponds, wildflower areas within site).
  • Amenity: Provides local green space and recreational activity.

Technical Guidance (UK Context - aligned with GI Guide principles)

Allotments provide valuable community assets. Design should consider access, water provision, soil quality, security, and integration of sustainable practices. Refer to National Allotment Society guidance.

  • Site Selection & Planning: Choose accessible sites with good sunlight. Consider existing land use, soil quality/contamination (remediation may be needed), water availability, security, and potential impact on neighbours. Plan layout with appropriate plot sizes, pathways (accessible design), communal areas, water points, tool storage, composting areas, and vehicle access for deliveries/maintenance.
  • Soil Management: Assess existing soil. Import good quality topsoil (BS 3882) if necessary. Encourage plot holders to improve soil fertility using organic methods (composting, green manures).
  • Water Provision: Essential requirement. Mains water supply usually needed, distributed via standpipes. Promote water efficiency (water butts on sheds, mulching). Consider potential for larger scale rainwater harvesting from adjacent buildings or SuDS integration (e.g., swale conveying treated water for dipping - requires water quality assessment).
  • Boundaries & Security: Define site boundary (fencing, hedging - native species preferred for biodiversity). Secure gates needed. Consider internal plot demarcation (low hedges, paths).
  • Communal Facilities: Provide space for tool sheds, composting bins (communal scheme), noticeboards, potentially a meeting area/shelter.
  • Sustainable Practices: Encourage organic growing methods, composting, water conservation, peat-free growing media, and wildlife-friendly management (e.g., pollinator planting, log piles, small ponds).
  • Management & Community Rules: Establish clear management structure (often local authority or allotment association) and tenancy rules covering cultivation standards, waste disposal, water use, pest control, etc.

Products Needed (Aligned with GI Guide principles)

  • Topsoil (BS 3882 compliant)
  • Soil Improver / Compost
  • Water Standpipes / Troughs
  • Water Butts (Large capacity)
  • Security Fencing & Gates
  • Path Materials (e.g., Woodchip, Aggregate)
  • Communal Tool Shed
  • Communal Composting Bins
  • Native Hedging Plants (for boundaries)
  • Site Signage / Noticeboard
