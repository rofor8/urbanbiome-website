Intensive Green Roof

Deeper soil layer (>150mm substrate), often accessible as a roof garden/park. Higher maintenance. (Sources: IGNITION, NE GIDG 4.5)

Key Benefits

  • Energy Saving (Below): 6.7% (or better)
  • Noise Reduction: 46 db
  • Rainwater Runoff Retained: 73%
  • Surface Temperature Reduction: 14.5°C
  • Air Temperature Reduction (Above): 1.1°C
  • Property Value Uplift (Accessible): 6.9%
  • (Carbon sequestration/storage and water quality benefits likely higher than extensive roofs)

Technical Guidance (UK Context)

  • Structural Assessment: EVEN MORE CRITICAL than extensive roofs. Intensive systems have much higher saturated weights (typically 200-500+ kg/m², potentially much more depending on features). Detailed structural engineering assessment and potential strengthening of the existing roof structure are almost always required. Load calculations must account for substrate, vegetation (including mature trees if planned), water features, paving, furniture, and occupant load.
  • Waterproofing & Root Barrier: As per extensive roofs, but requirements are even stricter due to deeper substrate and more vigorous plant roots. A high-performance, root-resistant waterproofing membrane (or a standard membrane with a separate robust root barrier) is essential. Meticulous detailing around penetrations, edges, and upstands is vital. Flood testing is recommended before installing other layers.
  • Drainage & Irrigation: A high-capacity drainage layer (e.g., deeper drainage boards, gravel layers) is needed to handle potentially larger water volumes and deeper substrates. An irrigation system (e.g., drip irrigation) is usually essential for intensive roofs in the UK climate, especially if lawns, perennials, shrubs, or trees are included. Ensure adequate water supply and pressure. Drainage outlets must be robust and accessible for maintenance.
  • Substrate: Deeper engineered substrate (typically 150mm to over 1m deep, depending on planting). Needs to balance water retention, drainage, aeration, stability, and nutrient content while minimising weight. Composition varies based on planting but often includes aggregates, sand, and organic matter. Different substrate depths may be used in different zones for varied planting.
  • Planting: Much wider range of plants possible compared to extensive roofs, including lawns, perennials, shrubs, and even small trees (subject to structural capacity and substrate depth). Plant selection should still consider wind exposure, sunlight, and desired maintenance levels. UK native species can be incorporated for biodiversity value.
  • Hard Landscaping & Features: If intended as accessible space, incorporate paving, seating, planters, lighting, water features etc. All these elements add significant load and require careful integration with the green roof system (drainage, waterproofing). Ensure accessibility compliance if required (e.g., Part M of Building Regs).
  • Edge Detail & Safety: Robust edge protection (e.g., parapet walls, railings meeting safety standards) is essential for accessible roofs. Vegetation-free zones are still required around edges and penetrations.
  • Maintenance: Significantly higher than extensive roofs, comparable to ground-level gardens or parks. Requires regular mowing (if lawn included), weeding, pruning, irrigation system checks, feeding, pest/disease monitoring, and inspection of drainage/structural elements.

Products Needed

  • High-Performance Waterproofing Membrane
  • Root Barrier Membrane (Heavy Duty)
  • Drainage/Reservoir Board (High Capacity)
  • Filter Fleece / Geotextile
  • Intensive Green Roof Substrate
  • Vegetation (Plants, Shrubs, Turf, Small Trees)
  • Irrigation System (Drip lines, controllers, etc.)
  • Edge Retainment / Railings / Parapet Components
  • Paving / Decking Systems (for accessible areas)
  • Gravel/Pebbles (for margins)
  • Drainage Outlets / Inspection Chambers
  • Roof Furniture, Planters (Optional)
